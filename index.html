Blockchain and cryptocurrency

Cryptocurrencies has been around since since late 2008, invented by an unidentified individual that goes by the alias Satoshi Nakamoto by introducing bitcoin and blockchain. The inventor’s goal was to develop an electronic cash system, using peer-to-peer network to prevent double-spending. (Nakamoto 2009) Since the invention of bitcoin, there are now over 900 different cryptocurrencies as of September 2017. (Brown 2017)

Cryptocurrencies have similarities to traditional money that we use on a day to day basis as they also work as a medium of exchange. Cryptocurrency is all facilitated, authorized and logged in a technology known as the blockchain technology. Just like in a bank, all transactions needs to be recorded and for cryptocurrencies, blockchain is essentially the financial ledger to record transactions but this is done differently. Blockchain is decentralized, without a dedicated server or central authority but is instead stored on thousands of cryptocurrency user’s computers around the world which are under the same network. (Beigel 2016) This allows cryptocurrency to have a public transaction history which make it technically impossible to counterfeit. (TechQuickie 2014) But despite blockchain being available to public, users have complete anomility as blockchain doesn’t contain any personal information.

There are a few ways in which cryptocurrencies can be obtained. Firstly, cryptocurrencies can be obtained by exchanging them using traditional money or accepting it as a form of payment as it is a medium of exchange. Other than that, cryptocurrencies can be obtained by a process called ‘mining’. This works by using a specialized software to solve complex mathematical problems and when a ‘miner’ finds the solution is awarded an amount of a certain cryptocurrency. Most ‘mining’ that happens presently are done by “pools”, which is basically a group of miners, combining their computing power to increase the chance of getting this reward when they solve the problem as a “pool”. When these “pools” of miners are big enough to a point that they control more than half of the network’s solving capacity which is known as a “51% attack”. (L.S. 2015) As a “pool” is able to gain more than 50% of the network, they are able to take control of the networks and reverse transactions, double-spend coins and halt the payments between users. (Investopedia n.d.)

As of now, cryptocurrencies aren’t widely acceptable yet but are on the rise with more and more merchants accepting certain cryptocurrency as a payment. Cryptocurrencies also have different laws in different countries, whilst many countries allowed it, some chose to ban them completely and others approve trading and holding them, but banned as a payment method. In the future, cryptocurrency might replace national currencies and could be replacing around 25% of the national currencies by 2030 according to Thomas Frey.

One of the reasons that cryptocurrencies has gain such popularity is due to the fact that most things are now done electronically and with the use of the advancing technology lead to the invention of blockchain which has now allowed money to be done digitally as well. Besides this, there are individual who seeks more freedom and hates the control of the government, this has lead them to use cryptocurrency with the anonymility it provides. (Argiro 2018) In recent years, cryptocurrency and blockchain has impacted the world in many ways and is likely to keep impacting the world, in both good and bad ways. Blockchain could be seen as a problem in the banking industry as they blockchain could replace banks as they both work as a ledger for financial transactions. (Schrodt 2018) As cryptocurrency has their transaction ledger decentralized on a massive scale, cryptocurrency doesn’t have any recourse to the US dollar which is the reserve currency of the global economy. Because of this, the mechanics of international trade, foreign relations, diplomacy and economic sanction will be impacted and cause the global economy to be “de-dollarize” which means that cryptocurrency could takeover the US dollar as the reserve currency of the global economy. Autonomous Weapons

As the name states, these machines are programmed and are able to carry out destructive task with the possibility of it causing harm to people.

“Killer robots” are created through a mix of Autonomous Systems and Artificial Intelligence. Autonomous systems are machines that are capable of interacting with the real world such as robots and drones. Artificial Intelligence is the simulation of human conscience or thinking in the form of technology. As machines such as self-flying drones are programmed with Artificial Intelligence, they are capable of undertaking tasks in the stead of humans and they are also able to make decisions based on their environment at hand.

The most evident use of this technology is mainly through automated drones (self-flying drones) or in military activities in warfare (automated weapon systems). Self-flying drones are used in delivery services such as Amazon to deliver parcels to receivers instead of having a human drive and drop off the package at the residence. These autonomous machines are able to “think” for themselves and choose the best option to undertake.

The technology is no longer in the development stage but now it is being able to be implemented into autonomous systems. There is always more development to be done to make the systems better however at the stage it is at now, there are more calls for autonomous weapons to be regulated or banned. The majority of the use of automated systems are for drones and driverless cars while there are also plans for more autonomous weapons which could prove lethal if unregulated.

Autonomous Vehicles

Competing companies have developed autonomous vehicles that do not entirely depend on human interaction to drive. Different types of autonomous cars rely on varying amounts of human assistance for its functioning. These driverless cars have the capability to detect their environment and with advanced technology systems, navigate the vehicle. A combination of software and sensors are utilised in the development to allow vehicles to be mainly controlled autonomously.

The technology that enables these vehicles to navigate without human input involve computer vision, laser lights, radar, global positioning system antennas, and odometry. Currently autonomous vehicles can employ cameras for computer vision to receive information on lane lines as well as the ability to locate surrounding cars. This equipment can detect the road traffic lights indicating vehicles whether to stop, slow down, or continue. A continuous stream of information is received by computer vision as the vehicle navigates within the environment.

Laser lights along with radars have been implemented within some vehicles to collect input of the environment. These components are used to further calculate specific measurements including velocity and distance between obstacles whilst driving. Various autonomous vehicles make use of ultrasonic sensors where a sound wave is released. An echo is produced when a wave contacts an object which then provides the vehicle with the exact obstacle position. This allows the vehicles to pinpoint the precise coordinates of obstructions, preventing possible collisions with surroundings.

Global positioning software is used for the vehicle to determine the best route when travelling to a certain destination and to provide an indication of where it is located. With a GPS, the software encoded in the vehicle can compute which route is most appropriate in terms of arrival times, amount of obstructions and main roads. The main human interaction that is essential for the functioning of autonomous cars is to input the destination through the system.

The development of autonomous vehicles is rapidly advancing due to competition between car and technology businesses. Despite the progress made within this industry, completely autonomous vehicles that have no reliance on human input are yet to be released. It is likely for one to be available for public use within the next few years as the technology for this task has been developed. The vehicles are undergoing extensive testing to ensure that it is safe to drive on public roads if it does rely solely on the system software.
